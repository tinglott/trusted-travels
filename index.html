Top Things to Do in Hilo, Hawaii

Hilo rewards curious travelers with an authentic Hawaiian experience refreshingly untouched by mass tourism. Whether you're chasing waterfalls through dense rainforest, peering into active volcanic craters, or paddling crystal-clear waters above volcanic reef, the Big Island activities available here could easily fill two weeks.

🌊 Natural Wonders & Waterfalls

Rainbow Falls sits just two miles from downtown and lives up to its name every sunny morning, when mist creates shimmering rainbows over the natural lava rock pool below. Arrive before 10 a.m. for the best display. Akaka Falls State Park is a non-negotiable stop — the park's signature cascade drops a jaw-dropping 442 feet into a lush, fern-lined gorge. A paved 0.4-mile loop trail winds through bamboo groves before delivering you to Akaka's thundering finale. Admission is $5 per vehicle.

For something wilder, head to Boiling Pots and Pe'epe'e Falls along the Wailuku River. The churning basalt pools create a bubbling, cauldron-like effect that's endlessly fascinating.

🌋 Hawaii Volcanoes National Park Day Trip

No trip to Hilo is complete without a visit to Hawaii Volcanoes National Park, just 30 miles southwest. This UNESCO World Heritage Site lets you walk across hardened lava fields, peer into the glowing Kilauea caldera, and experience the raw creative force of the Earth.

💡 Pro Tip Visiting the park is genuinely an all-day affair. When spectacular eruptions are occurring, it can extend well into the night — the glow of active lava against a dark Hawaiian sky is something no photograph fully captures.

Hiking Kilauea-Iki is the park's absolute highlight — this 4-mile loop descends into a solidified lava lake. The Thurston Lava Tube is a cathedral-like tunnel formed by flowing lava thousands of years ago. Don't miss the Chain of Craters Road — 19 miles descending 3,700 feet to the coastline past ancient petroglyphs, cinder cones, and dramatic ocean views. Park admission is $35 per vehicle, valid for seven days.

🐢 Beaches & Water Activities

Richardson Beach Park is the premier spot for snorkeling in Hilo. The protected black sand cove shelters colorful reef fish, Hawaiian green sea turtles, and spinner dolphins just yards from shore. Snorkel rentals run $10–$15 per day. Carlsmith Beach Park offers calm, protected swimming with a lifeguard on duty and frequent sea turtle sightings. Coconut Island (Mokuola), connected by a short footbridge, offers calm swimming with panoramic views of Mauna Kea.

🥾 Hiking & Adventure

The Onomea Trail through Hawaii Tropical Bioreserve & Garden is one of the most beautiful coastal paths anywhere. The Kaumana Caves offer free access to a massive lava tube system just four miles from downtown. For adrenaline seekers, Umauma Falls zipline (nine ziplines over three waterfalls) is an unforgettable $199 experience.

On clear nights, drive up to Mauna Kea for some of the finest stargazing on Earth. The summit road requires 4WD — the mid-level visitor station at 9,200 feet offers excellent viewing on its own.

🎭 Culture & History

The Merrie Monarch Festival, held the week after Easter, is the world's most prestigious hula competition — book accommodation up to a year in advance. The Pacific Tsunami Museum offers sobering context about the 1946 and 1960 tsunamis that devastated Hilo's bayfront. The Imiloa Astronomy Center brilliantly connects Native Hawaiian navigation traditions with telescope science — its planetarium shows alone are worth the $18 admission.

🚗 Scenic Drives

The Hamakua Coast Highway (Route 19) north of Hilo is one of Hawaii's most rewarding drives, tracing dramatic northeastern coastline through sugar plantation towns and tropical gulches. The 50-mile route culminates at Waipio Valley Lookout, where a sweeping panorama reveals a black sand beach and valley floor once home to Hawaiian royalty. And don't miss Banyan Drive — enormous banyan trees planted by Amelia Earhart and Babe Ruth form a cathedral canopy over Reeds Bay. Free, beautiful, and quintessentially Hilo.

Food & Dining

🍜 Hilo Food Scene: Where to Eat

The Hilo food scene is one of the most authentic and diverse on the entire Hawaiian archipelago — a delicious reflection of the island's multicultural roots. Start at the Hilo Farmers Market (Wednesdays and Saturdays along Kamehameha Avenue), packed with tropical fruits, local honey, and prepared foods. Arrive early — the best vendors sell out fast.

☕ Coffee Farm Tours

Kona & Ka'u estate tastings nearby

Try a loco moco at Cafe 100, widely credited as the birthplace of this iconic Hawaiian comfort dish — rice, hamburger patty, fried egg, and rich brown gravy. For something sweet, Two Ladies Kitchen makes legendary handcrafted mochi stuffed with fresh strawberries. Big Island Candies lets you watch artisans hand-dip chocolate shortbread in real time. And don't skip a coffee farm tour — several estates near Hilo offer tastings and behind-the-scenes looks at how Kona and Ka'u coffee goes from cherry to cup.

Accommodation

🏨 Where to Stay in Hilo

Figuring out where to stay in Hilo comes down to travel style and budget — the good news is the city offers options across the full spectrum. Downtown Hilo is ideal for walking to restaurants, shops, and the Farmers Market. Banyan Drive, often called Hilo's hotel row, lines scenic Waiakea Peninsula with mid-range properties offering gorgeous bay views. If you plan significant time at Hawaii Volcanoes National Park, consider staying closer to the park boundary.

⚠️ Important Note Short-term vacation rentals (Airbnb, VRBO) are not recommended in Hawaii because they have contributed to an extreme housing crisis on the islands. Stay in hotels and legal vacation condos — your travel dollars support sustainable local businesses and protect housing for residents.

Planning

☀️ Best Time to Visit & Weather Guide

Understanding Hilo weather before you book is essential — this is one of the rainiest cities in the United States. But with the right expectations and a good rain jacket, Hilo is magical year-round. The best time to visit is spring, March through May. Temperatures hover between a comfortable 75°F and 82°F, the landscape is lush and vibrantly green, and the Merrie Monarch Festival draws cultural practitioners from across Hawaii.

Rainfall is frequent but often short-lived — expect passing showers rather than days of unrelenting downpour. Mornings tend to be clearer, making them ideal for waterfalls, botanical gardens, and volcano hikes. Pack light layers, a compact umbrella, and quick-dry clothing. Embrace the rain — it's what keeps Hilo's waterfalls roaring and its rainforests impossibly beautiful.

Getting There

✈️ How to Get to Hilo: Flights & Budget Tips

Hilo International Airport (ITO) serves the east side of the Big Island, with connections from Los Angeles, Seattle, and San Francisco. Flights to Hilo have dropped approximately 26% year-over-year — an excellent moment to lock in cheap flights before prices rebound. Flexibility with travel dates (mid-week or spring shoulder season) can unlock even greater savings.

If you're already in Hawaii, inter-island flights from Honolulu to Hilo run frequently via Hawaiian Airlines and Southwest, with flight times under an hour. Once you arrive, park admission is $35 at the gate — and if you arrive late at night to see the stars or glowing lava, you might not pay any fee at all, making spontaneous midnight volcano runs one of the great free thrills the Big Island offers.
